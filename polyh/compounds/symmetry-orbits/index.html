Introduction

In the book [HVER01] Mr. Verheyen derives all possible groups of cube compounds. This page is based on that book. The idea to apply group algebra for listing all compounds of polyhedra is great, unfortunately the book is rather dry and hard to understand. I think that Verheyen was strong in the field of abstract theory, but he wasn't very good in explaining this to others. One problem is that the book starts with a lot of abstract definitions without examples and without any explanation of their need or future usage. Besides that there are some small mistakes that make it even harder for the reader sometimes. For this reason I decided to write this page: as a support to read this book. More about this below.

The first chapter of the book enumerates the symmetry groups in 3D Euclidean space. The symmetry groups are based directly on generic group algebra, not on the Schönflies groups, which are based on cristallography. This chapter is of high quality with very clear images and pictures.

The second chapter "Symmtry Action" contains a lot of abstract group algebra and is hard to understand even if you have some basic understanding of group algebra already. The problem that I had with this chapter was that it consists only of formal definitions and there aren't any examples.

If you want to explain to a young child what the square root of a number is and you start with giving some kind of formal definition like this: the square root of a number is that number that if you multiply it by itself then it will give the original number, then chances are that the child will not understand and just think that calculating a square root is very complicated. If on the other hand you start with some examples and say: the square root of four is two, since two times two equals to four and the square root of nine is three, since three times three equals to nine, and perhaps even another example with sixteen, then the child will probably know the answer when you ask what the square root of twentyfive is.

Most of us are good at recognising patterns: our brains see patterns even when there is only noise. Examples serve as the patterns to teach us the general rule, and it is good to start with examples when you try to learn or teach something new. In fact this is the base of science: you start with experimenting to get a feeling of how the something works, and then you try to come up with a general rule. After that you will challenge this general rule with even more experiments, but the fact remains the first experiments and the examples have the same purpose: to build a basic understanding of something new.

Obviously the formal definitions and formulas are an essential part as well. Their purpose is to make sure that everyone has the same understanding and use the exact same terms.

However, all too often these abstract formal definitions are used as the first introduction of a new subject. Though this might be enough for some, most of us need the examples to get a feeling about the subject. I think that in [HVER01] Verheyen made this classical mistake. Formal definitions of terms are given without any examples, and even without stating what this is going to be used for. With this page I hope I can provide some of examples, to help to understand what Verheyen tried to do.

The listing given here are suggestions of improvements in the book, including some mistakes that I found. These are not confirmed by the author and therefore they cannot be considered as any official errata. Since the author seemed to have passed away according to this article I decided to publish them here. I hope that it will help a reader to understand the book better.

  • Page 52: D4D2 has 2 reflections, not 3.
  • Page 53: The text 'Number of mirrors: 3 (Fig. 56a) + 1 (Fig. 56b)' could be misinterpreted. Fig. 56a itself doesn't show 3 mirrors, but there are 3 mirror planes of the type shown in Fig. 56a, where the plane contains one 3-fold and one 2-fold axis. The same improvement can be made when referring to similar images in the next symmetries.
  • Page 67: In the first sentence in section 1.1 'C' shouldn't be in bold font: it should 'C' instead.
  • Page 68: At item 3, it would be clearer to state that the two cosets are H and G - H
  • Page 75: In mapping t: G / Cs should be G / Gs. It could be helpful to mention that in the line below below for C · Gs that C ∈ G
  • Page 76 (1): In the first paragraph G1 should be Gs and p1 should be ps.
  • Page 76 (2): In case 3 the notation ⟨H, Gs ⟩ ⊂H means that this set is used as a generator; i.e. the isometries are recursively combined with each other until no new isometries are found. This is to close the group. However this would never lead to case 1 as the text says, but it is meant that this new group becomes an Fs, which takes the role of Gs in case 1 since H ⊂ Fs.
  • Page 76 (3): At the bottom of the page the symbol ⇋ is used. It would be good to mention what this symbol stands for. I think this symbol is meant to represent the 'equipotent' relation.
  • Page 77: In the figure title H1, H2, G1, and G2 should be H, Hs, G and Gs respectively, I think. In that case it fits with the text as well.
  • Page 78: At the top of the page there is a reference to 'Diagram 2'. That should be 'the diagram below'.
  • Page 125: In Section 4.2, item 1: Fig. 83 should be Fig. 81, and in item 2: Fig. 81 should be Fig. 82.
  • Page 126: At the top of the page Fig. 81 should be Fig. 83.
  • Page 135: Figure 100 and 101 show compounds of 6 cubes, not 2. I.e. 2 | A4 x I / C2 x I should be 6 | A4 x I / C2 x I (twice).
  • Page 157: 2n | D4n x I / C4n x I should be 2n | D4n x I / C4 x I and 2n | D3n x I / C3n x I should be 2n | D3n x I / C3 x I.
  • Page 197: In figure 135 it would have made more sense to switch left and right on the button row. Then Bakos' compound is always on the left and the Theosophical compound is on the right.
  • Page 140: In item 7: 2 | D2 x I / C2 x I should be 2n_D2nxI_D1xI.off
  • Page 172 (1): In the first paragraph of section 3.1 D6 x I / D3 x I should be D3 x I / C3 x I
  • Page 172 (2): In section 3.1 there is a reference to Fif. 119, but that figure doesn't show the intended compound. It can be seen in Fig. 96 on page 128 or in Fig. 121 on the left side.
  • Page 173: Wrong image in Fig. 119: it doesn't show 2 | D3 x I / C3 x I | 22°14'19".52, but the rigid 3 | D6 x I / D2 x I (neither does it show a compound along a three-fold axis).
  • Page 188: 20 | A5 x I / C3 x I | 31°02'41".91 should be 20 | A5 x I / C3 x I | 23°25'51".28
  • Page 214: In section 5.5: 4 | A4 x I / C2 x I should be 6 | A4 x I / C2 x I
  • Page 224: The year 1990 referring to Brückner should be 1900
