Introduction

This page summarises which polyhedra were found that only consist of regular folded heptagons with S4A4 symmetry. Look here for general background information.The listing is sub-divided into the different heptagon folds.

Heptagons folded using a Triangle Fold

3D Model Extra Info Link
This one is an exception. It was found while looking for A4 solutions for G-folds in position 4, but one of the angles become exactly 0 degrees so it becomes a triangle fold and by the way the heptagons are positioned reflections appear. One heptagon is folded into this and then the equilateral triangle bases are put on a tetrahedron covering each tetrahedron triangle three times in different positions. This means that there are three seperate edges for each tetrahedron edge. It is the 2-fold symmetry axis that defines which base components are connected. The three pairs at the tetrahedron edge closest to the viewer are , and . If you follow these connections, then you can actually see that this actually a compound of three with D4D2 symmetry.
Similarly to the previous one this one was found while looking for A4 solutions for G-folds in position 4. The same angle becomes exactly 0 degrees and the heptagons end up in positions so that reflections appear. Once again the heptagon is folded into this and even here the equilateral triangle bases are put on a tetrahedron, but this time the base components are facing the opposite direction. This means that this is a compound of three with D4D2 symmetry. If you compare this one with the previous polyhedron with D4D2 symmetry you can see that the orientation of the base components is switched.

Heptagons folded using a W Fold

3D Model Extra Info Link
Quite a simple polyhedron. Looking at the 3-fold axis one can easily imagine how the folded heptagon continues inside.
Similar to the previous one, but the heptagons are placed deeper in the polyhedron so that they stick out.
Looks like four sharp arrow points arranged in a tetrahedron symmetry. Quite a bit harder here to recognise the self-intersecting heptagons and also difficult to build with small dihedral angles and thin pieces.

Heptagons folded using a Shell Fold

3D Model Extra Info Link
Here the folded heptagons aren't full visible. If you look into a 3-fold axis, you can recognise three pairs of folded heptagons, but to connect to the next one through a real edges (not an intersection) you will need to skip one pair. This means you will circle twice arount the 3-fold axis to connect all. Inside there are once again flaps consisting of pieces of two different heptagons. Here these flaps point outwards, but cannot see them. This means that there is a variant that looks exactly the same from the outside, but with the flaps sticking inwards. That on isn't shown in this table.
This one looks very similar to 'S4A4 W fold no. 3', but they are different. Here there are flaps of two heptagon pieces sharing a plane. That is not the case for the other one.
This one is the same as the previous one, except that the flaps are folded inwards.
Another bizarre one with flaps. Here three different heptagon parts almost fall into the same plane.
This one is the same as the previous one, except that the flaps are folded inwards.
This one might look a bit complicated at first glance, but it isn't too difficult to see how the heptagons are oriented. Iy you look into a 3-fold axis there are three colours. All parts of one colour is part of one folded heptagon, for which you can see all folds. If you connect all heptagons around the 3-fold axis you will go around twice.
